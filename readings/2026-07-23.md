# Wings of Their Own: The Teens Who Fly Before They Drive

*Reading time: ~7 minutes · Level: Bagrut (Band III)*

Long before they are old enough for a driving test, some teenagers are already learning to fly. Every weekend, at a small airfield outside town, a group of 14- to 17-year-olds meet to train as glider pilots. Their program is run by a youth aviation club, but it gets support and equipment from the local **air force**, which lends old training gliders and sends experienced pilots to help teach. For these teens, flying is not a distant dream. It is a normal Saturday morning.

## A Different Kind of Weekend

Most of the cadets, as the young trainees are called, remember their first flight clearly. The glider is pulled into the air by a cable attached to a small plane, and once it is high enough, the cable is released. Suddenly there is no engine noise at all, only wind. "It's silent, and it's terrifying, and it's amazing, all at once," said one sixteen-year-old cadet. New students often need help to **cope** with that first rush of fear. Instructors say the trick is simple: **focus on** one thing at a time — the horizon, the speed, the next instruction — instead of everything at once.

Training does not begin in the air, though. Cadets spend hours in a classroom first, learning about weather, air pressure, and safety rules. Only after passing written tests are they allowed near the controls. The club's small **headquarters**, a converted shipping container beside the runway, is where lessons, maps, and old photographs of former cadets are kept. New members quickly learn that flying is really about patience.

## More Than a Certificate

After months of lessons, each cadet takes a final flight test with an instructor watching closely. If they pass, they receive an official **certificate** that proves they can fly a glider solo, without an instructor beside them. For many teens, this piece of paper becomes far more than a license. It is a **symbol** of independence, proof that they can be trusted with real responsibility years before they can legally drive a car.

The moment a cadet lands after their first solo flight is always celebrated the same way. Friends and instructors gather by the runway, someone rings an old bell, and everyone shouts "**Cheers!**" as the new pilot climbs out of the cockpit, usually smiling and a little shaky. It is a small tradition, but it matters. It tells every nervous beginner that the whole club remembers what that first solo flight felt like.

## Passing the Controls

Not every cadet stays for the flying alone. Over time, many build **strong** friendships with people they would never have met at school. Younger and older cadets train together, and the older ones often become informal mentors, showing beginners how to check equipment or read a weather report. This year, the club decided to **extend** its program to include thirteen-year-olds for the first time, so students can start learning the theory even earlier, before they are tall enough to reach the pedals.

Change is also happening at the top of the club. After eleven years as head instructor, one of the founding members recently stepped down. A former cadet, now twenty-four and a licensed commercial pilot, agreed to **take her place**, becoming the youngest head instructor the club has ever had. "I was scared on my first flight, just like everyone else," she said. "Now I get to be the voice telling someone else that they can do it."

## Why It Matters

Programs like this exist in many countries, often connecting young people with careers in aviation, engineering, or the military. But most cadets say the biggest lesson is not really about flying at all. It is about discovering, at fifteen or sixteen, that you can learn something genuinely difficult and dangerous-looking, and do it safely, carefully, and well. That confidence, instructors say, tends to follow cadets long after they leave the airfield — into exams, job interviews, and every other challenge that once looked impossible from the ground.
