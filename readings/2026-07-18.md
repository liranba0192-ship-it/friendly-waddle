# Plastic That Destroys Itself

*Reading time: ~7 minutes · Level: Bagrut (Band III)*

Plastic is everywhere — in food packaging, phone cases, and even clothing. For decades, most people have shared the same **dislike**: plastic waste piles up in oceans and landfills and takes hundreds of years to disappear. Recycling helps, but only a small share of plastic is ever actually recycled, and the rest keeps piling up year after year. But scientists have just announced something that sounds like science fiction. They have created a new kind of plastic that can destroy itself completely, using tiny living bacteria hidden inside the material.

## A Growing Problem

Global plastic production has seen a **steep** rise over the past fifty years, and most of it never truly disappears. It only breaks into smaller and smaller pieces, called microplastics, that spread through soil, rivers, and even the food we eat. Studies suggest that ordinary plastic bags and bottles can take centuries to break down naturally, long after the products they once protected are forgotten. This slow decay has pushed researchers to **hunt** for a better solution — one that does not simply hide the problem but actually solves it. For years, most attempts focused on recycling old plastic or switching to paper alternatives, but neither idea removed plastic waste on a large scale.

## How the New Plastic Works

The new material is, **essentially**, ordinary plastic with a surprising twist: engineered bacteria are mixed directly into it while it is being made. As long as the plastic stays dry and sealed **tight**, the bacteria remain asleep, and the material behaves like normal plastic — strong, flexible, and safe to use. It can be shaped into bottles, wrappers, or bags without losing any of its everyday usefulness. But when the plastic is exposed to a specific trigger, such as moisture or a special nutrient, the bacteria wake up. They begin eating the plastic from the inside, breaking it down into harmless natural compounds. Remarkably, the whole process can be finished in about six days, with no toxic waste left behind and nothing harmful released into the soil or water nearby. Scientists say the material even has the **capacity** to be adjusted, so that it lasts longer or shorter depending on how it will be used — a food wrapper, for **exactly** one example, could be designed to last only until the next garbage collection day, while a container built for storage might survive much longer before it is triggered.

## From Lab to Kitchen

Researchers first tested the bacteria in small samples before building a full **catalog** of possible materials, each with a different mix of ingredients and speeds. Every experiment was carefully recorded, and the data was stored and shared on university **servers** so teams around the world could compare results and avoid repeating the same tests. The next challenge is moving the technology from the laboratory to everyday products — bags, cups, and packaging that people use in their kitchens every day. This step is not simple: the plastic has to remain cheap enough to produce, safe enough for food contact, and just as reliable as the plastic we already use. Manufacturers will also need to be convinced that switching production lines is worth the cost.

## What Comes Next

**Even if** this new plastic is not perfect yet, many scientists believe it marks an important turning point. Instead of only recycling or reducing plastic use, we may soon be able to grow materials that clean up after themselves. Of course, questions remain about cost, safety, and how quickly the technology can reach ordinary stores, and it may take several more years of testing before it appears on supermarket shelves. Still, for a planet tired of drowning in waste, a plastic that quietly disappears when its job is done offers a rare reason for hope. Young people who grow up more aware of environmental issues than earlier generations may be among the first to demand that such inventions leave the laboratory and reach everyday life, from school cafeterias to local supermarkets.
