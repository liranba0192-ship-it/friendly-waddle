# Meet the Teenage Chairperson Running the Show

*Reading time: ~7 minutes · Level: Bagrut (Band III)*

In many high schools around the world, students are no longer just following rules that adults made for them. More and more schools now let students run their own clubs and councils, with a real budget, real meetings, and a real teenager in charge. It might sound **weird** at first — a sixteen-year-old making decisions that used to belong to teachers — but this kind of leadership is becoming more common every year, and many educators believe it teaches skills that no textbook can.

## A New Kind of Leader

At one school in the north, a tenth grader named Noa was elected **chairperson** of the student council last year. Her job was not just to plan parties. She had to run meetings, listen to complaints, and decide how to spend the council's small budget. At first, some teachers worried that a student would not have the right **personality** for such a serious role. But Noa turned out to be calm under pressure and good at listening, exactly the qualities the job needed.

"People expect a leader to be loud," Noa says. "But mostly I just needed to **assure** everyone that their ideas would actually be heard, not just written down and forgotten."

## From a Weird Idea to a Real Plan

Every year, students bring dozens of suggestions to the council, and most of them never go anywhere. Last spring, one ninth grader **proposed** something unusual: a joint weekend event with a partner school in another city, where students from both schools would **stay over** at each other's homes for two nights. The idea sounded ambitious, maybe even impossible to organize. But instead of rejecting it, the council decided to test it on a small scale first, with just twenty students from each side.

The event needed **generous** families willing to open their homes to strangers, and it needed students willing to **speak about** their own school's problems honestly with people they had just met. Both things happened. Families offered spare rooms without being asked twice, and students who normally stayed quiet in class suddenly had a lot to say when talking to visitors from another town.

## More Than Just a Title

Running a council teaches students to **spot** problems before they become big ones. When the joint weekend event first started, a few students clearly felt lonely and out of place among strangers. Noa noticed this early and quickly paired them with more outgoing classmates, which put everyone **in** a much better mood by the second day. Small adjustments like this rarely make headlines, but they often decide whether a project succeeds or quietly falls apart.

Teachers who supervise these councils say the biggest change is not in what students plan, but in how they think. Students learn to manage disagreements, keep promises, and take responsibility when something goes wrong — skills that matter far beyond school.

## Why It Matters

Studies on youth leadership suggest that students who take on real responsibility early tend to feel more confident handling challenges later in life, whether at university or at work. A title like "chairperson" is not just decoration for a resume. It comes with real pressure, real mistakes, and real lessons.

Noa's term as chairperson ends this summer, and a new student will take over the role. She says she is proud, but also relieved. "It taught me that leadership isn't about being the loudest person in the room," she says. "It's about making sure everyone else feels comfortable enough to speak up too."
